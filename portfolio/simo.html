Simplified Information for Mobility and Orientation

June 2014

User research Accessibility

This was an Australian Research Council linkage project. I teamed up with a group of researchers to explore the development of a Mobility and Location Information System for the Blind and Visually Impaired (BVI).

The challenge

The internal perception of surroundings by a person with limited or no sight is remarkably different to our own. How do we design an interface which delivers information to this particular group of users in the most accessible and meaningful way?

Solutions

Interviewing and researching user behaviour

Literature reviews and interviews were undertaken to understand the cognitive process of BVI users when attempting to navigate an unfamiliar environment. It has been found that associating landmarks with such areas is generally the most effective means of developing spatial awareness and remembering a route or set of directions.

Putting research into practice

An initial prototype was built around those theoretical findings. We used wireframes, screenreaders, video recordings, anything that would capture users response in real life and identify pain points.

Forming a decison matrix

I established a decision matrix to prioritise which stories should be included in the delivery pipeline. This gave us a bettter overview of the crossover between engineering stories such as 'accuracy','robustness, and user experience stories such as 'integration with the environment' and ‘providing quality information’.

Results

Usability metrics such as success rates and task times were significantly better than similar products. Users felt more confident using the prototype than their existing assisstive devices.

The design framework and recommendations were published. Our team continued to raise awareness for mobile accessibility at major conferences and events.
